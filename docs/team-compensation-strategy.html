Founding Team & Compensation Strategy

Founding Team

The Owl's Eye was conceived, designed, and built by three co-founders whose combined expertise spans investigation, AI engineering, and scalable systems architecture—allowing the platform to progress from concept to live deployment without outside capital.

Alexandra Solórzano — Founder, CEO & Lead Investigator. Licensed private investigator with over ten years of experience in federal criminal defence, white collar crime, and financial fraud. Alexandra is the domain authority behind the platform—translating real-world investigative demands into product design and ensuring The Owl's Eye solves the problems practitioners actually face.

Neil Byrne — Co-Founder & CTO. Solutions engineer with over ten years across data engineering, AI systems, and full-stack development. Responsible for platform architecture, AI pipeline design, and technical strategy. Neil drives the technology vision that underpins the platform's intelligence capabilities and competitive differentiation.

Conor Bowles — Co-Founder & Head of Engineering. Software engineer specialising in data infrastructure, security, and scalable systems. Responsible for core platform engineering, database architecture, and deployment operations. Conor ensures the product is reliable, secure, and deployable within client environments at enterprise standards.

Compensation Strategy

Our approach to founding team compensation reflects a deliberate and capital-efficient transition strategy designed to preserve runway while ensuring the platform has the daily engineering capacity it needs to operate as a commercial product. Within the $1.5M seed round, total R&D allocation stands at $750K, of which founding team compensation represents $480K—split between the CEO at $180K, the CTO at $180K, and an Engineering allocation of $120K over an 18–24 month runway. These figures are structured to accommodate a phased commitment model rather than placing all three founders on full-time salaries from day one.

Conor Bowles transitions to full-time immediately. A commercial product requires someone whose entire working day is dedicated to the platform: shipping features, onboarding customers technically, managing production infrastructure, and responding to issues in real time. As Head of Engineering, Conor becomes the daily engine of the operation—the person who keeps the product moving forward at the pace paying customers expect. His full-time rate is set at approximately $5,500–$6,500 per month, a startup-weighted rate that reflects both the equity position he holds as a co-founder and the reality of early-stage compensation. This is roughly 55–65% of the market rate for an equivalent infrastructure and full-stack engineering role in the Washington, DC market.

Neil Byrne remains part-time initially, with a defined transition trigger. The CTO allocation of $180K matches the CEO allocation, reflecting the seniority and strategic importance of the role. During the part-time phase, the draw is structured at approximately $10,000 per month, ensuring consistent availability for the work that requires his ten years of experience: architecture decisions, AI pipeline strategy, sprint planning, code reviews, and major feature design. These are the high-leverage tasks that shape the product's technical trajectory but do not yet require forty hours per week. The R&D contingency of $75K is deliberately structured to serve as the reserve for Neil's transition to full-time. If by month eight to ten the company has ten or more paying customers and the development backlog is growing faster than Conor and a junior developer can handle, the transition is triggered. The contingency covers the incremental cost of moving from part-time to full-time commitment, providing a clear financial path without requiring additional fundraising.

Alexandra Solórzano operates as full-time CEO from the outset. With the platform entering its commercial phase, the CEO role encompasses investor relations, sales leadership, customer relationships, and the ongoing translation of investigative domain expertise into product direction. Her allocation of $180K over the runway period represents a founder-rate salary that is significantly below market for a CEO role at a venture-backed company, consistent with the team's commitment to capital efficiency.

The trigger for the CTO going full-time is clearly defined: when customer onboarding demand, feature requests, or AI pipeline complexity requires more than fifteen to twenty hours per week of CTO-level work. Based on our projections, this is likely to occur around the ten to fifteen customer mark, or when the platform begins building domain-specific profiles for new verticals such as healthcare fraud or AML compliance. Investors should note that this is a disciplined approach—founders have thought carefully about when each transition makes financial and operational sense rather than assuming everyone goes full-time on day one.

Founder Economics

Below-Market Commitment

The combined founding team draw of approximately $480K over an 18–24 month runway represents roughly 45–55% of the market rate for the roles being performed. At equivalent market compensation in the Washington, DC area, a CEO, CTO, and Head of Engineering would collectively command $400K–500K per year—meaning the founding team is effectively contributing $350K–500K in foregone salary over the runway period alone.

This is in addition to the engineering value already built. The Owl's Eye platform—spanning a hybrid AI/graph intelligence engine, full-stack web application, document ingestion pipeline, and on-premise deployment architecture—would have cost an estimated $500K–$800K to develop at market engineering rates. The founding team built it for a fraction of that cost because they believed in it. Combined with the below-market salaries going forward, total founder contribution exceeds $1M before any outside capital is deployed.

This level of founder commitment is exactly what seed investors look for. It demonstrates conviction in the product, discipline in capital allocation, and a team that treats investor capital as a multiplier on their own investment rather than a substitute for it. Every dollar of the $1.5M seed round is directed toward commercial acceleration—scaling what already works—not toward building something from scratch or paying founders at market rates.
